Client: Northwave Outdoor Apparel
Project: Spring/Summer 2027 Campaign Kickoff
Date: 3 April 2026
Attendees: Sarah Howell (Northwave, Head of Brand), Marcus Lee (Northwave, Product Director), Priya Nair (Agency, Account Lead), Dom Walsh (Agency, Creative Director), Jess Okafor (Agency, Senior Strategist)

---

Priya: OK, so let's get into it. We've got about ninety minutes and I want to make sure we leave time for questions at the end. Sarah, do you want to kick us off with the brief overview before we get into specifics?

Sarah: Sure. So Northwave has had a strong few years in technical performance gear — trail running, alpine, that kind of thing. But we've been losing ground in what I'd call the aspirational outdoor space. That's the person who does a big hike once a month, maybe does a ski trip in January, buys quality gear but isn't obsessive about technical specs. They care how it looks. They care about values. And right now, our brand doesn't really speak to them.

Marcus: The product line is there. We launched the Meridian collection last autumn — lighter fabrics, cleaner aesthetics, still fully weatherproof. But it's sitting on shelves because people don't know it exists or they assume it's the same technical stuff we always do.

Dom: So the gap is really awareness and perception rather than product.

Marcus: Exactly. The product team has done the work. We need the narrative to catch up.

Jess: Can you tell us more about who you think this aspirational outdoor customer actually is? Because I think there's a real question about whether it's a demographic thing or more of a mindset.

Sarah: Good question. Our research points to mid-thirties to early fifties, probably skewing slightly female, urban or suburban but with strong nature-oriented identity. They read Monocle, they shop at Cos, they book a walking holiday in Umbria. They're not buying a jacket because it's thirty percent lighter — they're buying it because it makes them feel like a certain version of themselves.

Dom: So it's fundamentally about identity. The gear is a signal.

Sarah: Right. And Northwave has almost no presence there. If you ask someone in that demographic to name an outdoor brand they love, they'll say Arc'teryx or Patagonia. We don't register.

Priya: What's the ambition for the campaign in terms of shift? Are we going for broad awareness, or is this more targeted — building community?

Sarah: Both, honestly, but if I had to prioritise I'd say awareness first. We need to put ourselves on the map before we can build depth. So it's: exists, looks like this, stands for this. That needs to be clear before we can have more nuanced conversations.

Jess: And what does Northwave stand for, in your own words? What's the thing that's true about the brand that you want more people to know?

Sarah: That's the interesting one. I think it's something around the idea that nature is for everyone, not just the elite. There's a gatekeeping culture in outdoor — the right gear, the right trails, the right level of experience. Northwave's origin is actually in making mountaineering accessible; the founders started the company in the seventies to make technical gear affordable. That story has got lost.

Marcus: We do still do that. Our price points undercut Arc'teryx by thirty to forty percent for comparable quality. But we don't shout about it.

Dom: There's something really interesting there. Democratisation of the outdoors. Feels timely given broader conversations about access to green space, urban nature, that kind of thing.

Jess: Yes, and it differentiates against Patagonia, which has leaned heavily into environmentalism. This is more about human access than environmental activism, though obviously those things intersect.

Sarah: We care about sustainability — it's built into our supply chain — but that's table stakes now. I don't think we win on that positioning. The accessibility angle feels more ownable and more relevant to where culture is going.

Priya: So to summarise what I'm hearing: the campaign needs to reframe Northwave as the outdoor brand for people who love nature but don't want to be judged for not being hardcore about it. Quality gear, accessible ethos, and a cleaner aesthetic that signals taste as much as function.

Sarah: That's a really good summary. Yes.

Dom: I want to push on the aesthetic piece because it impacts creative direction significantly. Are we talking evolution of the current visual language, or is this a more significant rebrand moment for the campaign?

Marcus: We're not rebranding. The logo stays, the brand colours stay. But we have latitude within that — photography style, tone of voice, how we cast, where we show the product. That's all open.

Dom: Good. Because I think there's a real opportunity in the photography. A lot of outdoor advertising is all about performance — extreme conditions, the perfect summit shot. I think there's a more interesting story in the in-between moments. The early morning, the lunch stop, the walk back. That's where people actually live in outdoor experiences.

Sarah: I love that. It feels honest.

Jess: It also gives us a lot more space for diverse casting. The epic summit shot tends to look the same. The quieter moments can look like everyone.

Priya: What are the key campaign outputs we're working toward? Let's get practical.

Sarah: Lead visual for print and outdoor. Social suite — probably six hero assets, mix of static and short-form video. A hero film, around sixty to ninety seconds. And something for the website refresh — we're relaunching in June.

Priya: Timeline?

Marcus: We need everything locked by the end of July for autumn production. That means creative concepts signed off by end of May at the latest.

Dom: Tight but doable if we move quickly.

Priya: OK. We'll come back to you within two weeks with initial creative territories. Jess and her team will do some additional audience work first so we're grounding the concepts properly.

Sarah: Perfect. Really excited to see where this goes. I think there's something genuinely interesting here.
